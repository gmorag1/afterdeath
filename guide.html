📁 Document Locator

Gather these before you start. Check each one off as you find it — or note where it is.

You will need these documents many times across the weeks ahead — for the funeral home, SSA, insurance companies, banks, probate court, and more. Finding them now will save enormous time and stress later. Don't worry if you can't find everything immediately — an estate attorney can help locate missing items.

⏱️ First 24 Hours

Immediate actions that need to happen right away

⚕️
Legal Pronouncement of Death
Obtain the official legal pronouncement
NOT STARTED
▼
If your loved one passed in a hospital or under hospice care, the attending physician will issue the pronouncement and sign the death certificate. If the death was unexpected or occurred at home, call 911 immediately — paramedics or a coroner will handle the legal pronouncement. Do not move the body until authorities or the funeral home arrive. If the death occurred under suspicious circumstances, law enforcement will be involved automatically.
🏛️
Contact a Funeral Home
Arrange transport and begin planning
NOT STARTED
▼
The funeral home will coordinate transport of your loved one from the place of death to their facility. You do not need to choose a funeral home permanently at this stage — the initial transport can be arranged with any licensed funeral home, and you can transfer later if needed. Ask about their pricing (they are legally required to provide a General Price List under the FTC Funeral Rule). If cost is a concern, ask about direct cremation ($2,200 average) as the most affordable option. Bay Area funeral homes typically charge $2,200–$12,000 depending on services.
📞 What to say when you call
"Hi, I need to arrange transport for someone who has just passed away. My name is [your name]. The death occurred at [location — home, hospital, address]. Can you tell me your availability and the cost for transport? I'd also like to receive your General Price List — I understand you're required to provide it."

👥
Notify Immediate Family
Reach out to family and close friends
NOT STARTED
▼
Start with the closest family members — spouse, children, parents, siblings — and then move outward to extended family and close friends. It's okay to ask one trusted person to help make calls on your behalf. Consider creating a group text or using a platform like CaringBridge to share updates without repeating yourself. If the deceased had a religious community, notify the clergy or spiritual leader who may help coordinate communication.
📄
Locate Key Documents
Find the will, insurance policies, and estate papers
NOT STARTED
▼
Look for these critical documents: the will or living trust, life insurance policies, bank and investment account statements, Social Security card, birth certificate, marriage certificate, military discharge papers (DD-214 if veteran), property deeds, vehicle titles, and recent tax returns. Check the deceased's home safe, filing cabinet, desk, and safety deposit box. If the deceased had an attorney, contact them — they may have copies. Don't worry if you can't find everything immediately; the estate attorney can help locate missing documents.
🔒
Secure the Property
Protect the home, vehicle, and belongings
NOT STARTED
▼
Lock the deceased's home and ensure it's secure. Remove any perishable food if no one will be staying there. Collect mail daily to prevent identity theft. If the deceased lived alone, consider having a family member stay at the home or ask a trusted neighbor to keep watch. Do not distribute any belongings or personal items yet — this should wait until the estate is formally settled. Notify the home insurance company that the property may be temporarily unoccupied.
💼
Notify Employers
Contact the deceased's and your own employer
NOT STARTED
▼
Call the deceased's employer to notify them of the death. Ask about: any final paycheck or unpaid wages owed, life insurance benefits through their workplace (many employers provide a basic policy), pension or retirement account beneficiary procedures, and any personal belongings at their workplace. Also contact your own employer to arrange bereavement leave — most companies offer 3-5 days, and you may be able to use additional PTO or request unpaid leave if needed.
📞 What to say to the deceased's HR department
"Hello, I'm calling to notify you of the passing of [full name], who was an employee in [department/role]. They passed away on [date]. I need to speak with someone in HR about their final paycheck, any group life insurance benefits, retirement account beneficiary procedures, and how to collect their personal belongings. Who is the right person to speak with?"

📅 Days 2–7

Important planning and notification tasks

🕊️
Arrange the Service
Plan the funeral, memorial, or celebration of life
NOT STARTED
▼
Work with your chosen funeral home to plan the service. Key decisions include: burial vs. cremation, type of ceremony (religious, secular, or celebration of life), venue (funeral home chapel, place of worship, outdoor venue, or private home), date and time, who will officiate, and music/readings. If the deceased left written wishes, follow them as closely as possible. Don't feel pressured to decide everything at once — it's okay to hold a simple immediate service and plan a larger memorial for later.
📋
Order Death Certificates
Request 10-15 certified copies
NOT STARTED
▼
Order certified copies of the death certificate from your county's vital records office (the funeral home can usually handle this for you). You'll need more copies than you think — typically 10 to 15. Each institution (bank, insurance company, Social Security, DMV, etc.) will require an original certified copy. In California, certified copies cost approximately $21 for the first copy and $6 for each additional copy. The funeral home typically orders these as part of their services.
✍️
Write the Obituary
Draft and publish a notice of passing
NOT STARTED
▼
An obituary typically includes: full legal name and any nicknames, date and place of birth, date and place of death, names of surviving family members, education and career highlights, hobbies and passions, details about the funeral/memorial service, and where to send donations in lieu of flowers (if applicable). You can publish in local newspapers (San Francisco Chronicle charges $300-600), on Legacy.com, and for free on social media. The funeral home can help with placement.
SF Chronicle → East Bay Times → San Jose Mercury News → Legacy.com (free) →
🛡️
Contact Life Insurance
File the initial claim
NOT STARTED
▼
Locate the life insurance policy (check the deceased's files, email, and bank statements for premium payments). Call the insurance company's claims line and have ready: the policy number, a certified death certificate, and the deceased's Social Security number. Most claims are paid within 30-60 days. If you can't find a policy, check with the deceased's employer (workplace policies are common), search the NAIC Life Insurance Policy Locator at https://eapps.naic.org/life-policy-locator, or check old tax returns for premium deductions.
📞 What to say to the insurance company
"Hi, I'm calling to report the death of a policyholder and begin a claim. The policyholder's name is [full name] and the policy number is [number]. They passed away on [date]. I am the [beneficiary / executor / spouse]. Can you walk me through the claims process and tell me exactly what documentation you need? I also want to confirm the beneficiary on file."
🏛️
Notify Social Security
Stop benefits and apply for survivor benefits
NOT STARTED
▼
Call the Social Security Administration at 1-800-772-1213 (TTY 1-800-325-0778). The funeral home may report the death automatically, but confirm with SSA directly. If the deceased was receiving Social Security benefits, any payment received for the month of death or after must be returned. Surviving spouses may be eligible for a one-time death benefit of $255 and ongoing survivor benefits. Bring the death certificate, Social Security numbers for both the deceased and the survivor, and marriage certificate.
📞 What to say to Social Security (1-800-772-1213)
"Hi, I'm calling to report the death of a Social Security recipient. The deceased's name is [full name] and their Social Security number is [xxx-xx-xxxx]. They passed away on [date]. I want to confirm that benefits have been stopped. I am the [surviving spouse / child / executor]. I'd also like to understand what survivor benefits I may be eligible for and how to apply."
Call SSA: 1-800-772-1213
🌸
Arrange Flowers & Catering
Order sympathy arrangements and plan the gathering
NOT STARTED
▼
Sympathy flower arrangements typically range from $75 for a small bouquet to $300+ for standing sprays and casket pieces. Many Bay Area florists offer same-day delivery to funeral homes. For the post-service gathering (repast, wake, or shiva), consider catering for the expected number of guests. Options range from simple deli trays ($8-15/person) to full catering ($25-50/person). Many communities organize meal trains — don't hesitate to accept help.
Browse Bay Area Florists →
💝
Set Up a Memorial Fund
Create a fundraising page if needed
NOT STARTED
▼
If funeral costs are a concern, there is absolutely no shame in asking for help. GoFundMe reports that funeral fundraisers raise an average of $4,500 and are among the most successful campaign types on the platform. Be specific about costs in your campaign description and share it through social media and family group chats. Alternatively, many funeral homes can set up a memorial donation fund directed to a charity in the deceased's name.
Start a GoFundMe →
🏦
Notify Financial Institutions
Contact banks, credit cards, and investment accounts
NOT STARTED
▼
Contact each bank, credit union, and credit card company where the deceased held accounts. You'll typically need a certified death certificate and proof that you're the executor or next of kin. Ask about: joint accounts (these typically pass to the surviving owner), individual accounts (these will be frozen and become part of the estate), automatic payments set up on the accounts, and any outstanding balances. Do NOT pay any debts from your own funds — the estate is responsible for the deceased's debts, not you personally.
📞 What to say to each bank or financial institution
"Hello, I'm calling to notify you of the death of an account holder. The account holder's name is [full name] and they passed away on [date]. I am the [executor / next of kin / surviving joint owner]. I need to understand the process for: freezing or accessing the account, identifying any automatic payments that need to be cancelled, and transferring or closing the account. Can you connect me with your estate services department?"

📆 Weeks 2–4

Legal, financial, and administrative follow-up

📝
File Life Insurance Claim
Submit the formal claim with documentation
NOT STARTED
▼
If you haven't already filed, gather the policy document, a certified death certificate, and the claim form from the insurance company. Most insurers allow you to file by mail, fax, or online. Benefits are typically paid within 30-60 days of receiving all required documentation. If the claim is denied or delayed, contact your state's Department of Insurance. If the policy is large, consider consulting a financial advisor before deciding how to receive the payout (lump sum vs. annuity).
⚖️
Contact an Estate Attorney
Begin the probate or trust settlement process
NOT STARTED
▼
If the deceased had a trust, an attorney can help you settle it without going through probate court — this is faster and more private. If there's only a will (or no estate plan), probate will likely be required. California probate typically takes 9-18 months and costs 4-7% of the estate's value in fees. Many Bay Area estate attorneys offer a free initial consultation. Bring the will/trust, death certificate, and a list of the deceased's assets and debts.
Find Estate Attorneys →
🚗
Cancel the Driver's License
Notify the DMV and handle vehicle registration
NOT STARTED
▼
Visit the California DMV or submit by mail: a certified death certificate, the deceased's driver's license (if available), and a completed REG 5 form for vehicle transfer. If the vehicle was jointly owned, it can transfer to the surviving owner with a new title. If there's an outstanding auto loan, contact the lender — the estate may need to continue payments or negotiate the loan. Consider whether the family wants to keep, sell, or donate the vehicle.
📱
Cancel Utilities & Subscriptions
Transfer or close recurring services
NOT STARTED
▼
Make a comprehensive list of all recurring payments and subscriptions. Check bank statements and credit card bills for the past 3 months to catch everything. Common items to cancel or transfer: utilities (PG&E, water, garbage), internet and phone, streaming services (Netflix, Spotify, etc.), gym memberships, magazine subscriptions, Amazon Prime, cloud storage, and any other auto-pay services. Some companies will issue refunds for unused portions; always ask.
📞 What to say to utilities and subscription companies
"Hi, I'm calling to cancel an account. The account holder, [full name], passed away on [date]. The account number is [number] and the service address is [address]. I have a death certificate available if required. I'd like to: cancel the service effective [date], receive confirmation in writing, and inquire about any refund for unused portions of a prepaid period. Who can help me with this?"
📬
Forward Mail
Set up USPS mail forwarding
NOT STARTED
▼
Visit your local post office or go to usps.com to set up mail forwarding from the deceased's address to yours (or the executor's address). This costs $1.10 online for identity verification. Mail forwarding lasts 12 months for most mail and 12 months for packages. This is important for catching any bills, financial statements, or legal notices that arrive. Watch for any unfamiliar correspondence — it may reveal accounts or obligations you weren't aware of.
📊
Plan for Taxes
Consult an accountant about the final tax return
NOT STARTED
▼
The deceased's final federal and state tax returns must be filed for the year of death. If the deceased was married, the surviving spouse can file a joint return for that year. You may also need to file an estate tax return (Form 706) if the estate exceeds the federal exemption ($13.61 million in 2024). California does not have a state estate tax. A CPA or tax attorney can help identify deductions (medical expenses, funeral costs) and ensure compliance. The filing deadline is April 15 of the year following death.
🎖️
File for Veteran Benefits
Apply for VA burial benefits if applicable
NOT STARTED
▼
If the deceased was a veteran, the surviving family may be eligible for: a burial allowance ($2,000+ for service-connected death, $948 for non-service-connected), a free burial in a national cemetery, a government headstone or marker (free), a Presidential Memorial Certificate, and a burial flag. Contact the VA at 1-800-827-1000 or visit va.gov. You'll need the DD-214 discharge papers, death certificate, and marriage certificate (if applying as spouse). Many veterans' service organizations (VFW, American Legion) offer free assistance with claims.
📞 What to say to the VA (1-800-827-1000)
"Hello, I'm calling to inquire about burial and survivor benefits for a recently deceased veteran. The veteran's name is [full name]. They served in the [Army/Navy/Air Force/Marines/Coast Guard] and were discharged on [approximate date]. They passed away on [date]. I am their [spouse/child/family member]. I have a copy of their DD-214 discharge papers. Can you tell me what benefits are available, which ones I'm eligible for, and walk me through the application process?"
Call VA: 1-800-827-1000
🏠
Update Property Titles
Transfer deeds, titles, and registrations
NOT STARTED
▼
Real property (homes, land) held in a trust can be transferred by the trustee recording a new deed. Property held in joint tenancy passes automatically to the surviving owner — file an Affidavit of Death of Joint Tenant with the county recorder. Property held solely by the deceased will go through probate. For vehicles, use the DMV REG 5 form. For bank and investment accounts, contact each institution with a death certificate and proof of authority (Letters Testamentary from probate court or a Certification of Trust). Keep records of everything.

🌱 Months 2–6

Estate settlement and personal healing

💳
Settle Outstanding Debts
Work with the estate to resolve loans and obligations
NOT STARTED
▼
Important: In most cases, you are NOT personally responsible for the deceased's debts. The estate pays debts from its assets. If creditors contact you, do not agree to pay anything from your own funds — refer them to the estate executor. Secured debts (mortgage, car loan) are tied to the asset; the estate can continue paying, sell the asset, or let the lender reclaim it. Unsecured debts (credit cards, medical bills) are paid from estate assets; if the estate doesn't have enough, those debts may go unpaid. Beware of debt collectors who pressure family members illegally.
📞 What to say if a creditor calls you
"Thank you for calling. I want to let you know that [full name] has passed away. I am not the executor or administrator of the estate, and I am not personally responsible for this debt. Please direct all further correspondence in writing to the estate executor, [name], at [address]. I am unable to authorize any payments or make any commitments on behalf of the estate at this time." Note: Under the FDCPA, debt collectors cannot legally pressure family members to pay a deceased person's debts from their own funds. If a collector is aggressive, you can report them to the CFPB at consumerfinance.gov.
📦
Distribute Assets
Follow the will or trust to distribute belongings
NOT STARTED
▼
Distribution should only happen after all debts, taxes, and administrative expenses are paid. If there's a will, follow its instructions. If there's no will, California's intestacy laws determine who inherits (generally: spouse first, then children, then parents, then siblings). For sentimental items not mentioned in the will, consider holding a family meeting to discuss fair distribution. Document everything distributed and have recipients sign acknowledgment forms. If disputes arise, a mediator can be less expensive and less damaging to family relationships than litigation.
📑
File the Final Tax Return
Submit federal and state returns for the year of death
NOT STARTED
▼
The final Form 1040 covers January 1 through the date of death. Report all income earned during that period. A surviving spouse can file jointly for the year of death and may qualify for 'qualifying surviving spouse' status for up to two additional years. The executor or administrator signs the return on behalf of the deceased (write 'DECEASED' and the date of death at the top of the return). If a refund is due, file Form 1310 to claim it. Attach a copy of the death certificate.
💻
Handle Social Media Accounts
Memorialize or close digital accounts
NOT STARTED
▼
Facebook allows you to memorialize the account (adds 'Remembering' before the name) or delete it — visit facebook.com/help/contact/305593649477238. Instagram also offers memorialization or removal. Google has an Inactive Account Manager that may have been set up. For email accounts, you may need a court order to access them. Cancel any social media accounts the deceased wouldn't have wanted to remain active. Also close or transfer: email accounts, cloud storage (iCloud, Google Drive, Dropbox), domain registrations, and cryptocurrency wallets (if the keys can be located).
💚
Seek Grief Support
Take care of yourself — it's not optional, it's essential
NOT STARTED
▼
Grief doesn't follow a timeline. Weeks or months after the logistics are handled, the emotional weight often hits hardest. Please consider: individual therapy with a grief-trained counselor, a grief support group (sharing with others who understand is powerful), your primary care physician if grief is affecting your sleep, appetite, or daily functioning, and crisis resources if you're having thoughts of self-harm (988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline — call or text 988). The Bay Area has excellent grief support resources, many of them free.
