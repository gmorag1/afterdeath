Local Support Services

These Bay Area organizations offer counseling, support groups, and compassionate care — many at no cost.

JFCS Grief & Bereavement Counseling

Counseling Support Groups Sliding Scale

Individual and group grief counseling serving San Francisco and Marin counties. All services welcome anyone regardless of age, faith, or type of loss. Offers specialized groups for spousal loss, parent loss, and sudden/traumatic death.

(415) 449-1212

By the Bay Health — Grief Support

Support Groups Free

Offers community grief support groups open to anyone in San Francisco, regardless of whether your loved one was in their hospice program. Groups meet regularly and provide a safe, facilitated space to share your experience with others who understand.

(415) 526-5699

KARA Grief Support

Children & Teens Adults Free

One of the Bay Area's most respected grief organizations, based in Palo Alto. Offers peer support groups for children (ages 5+), teenagers, and adults. KARA's model centers on community and shared experience rather than clinical therapy. All services are free.

(650) 321-5272

Bay Area CBT Center

Therapy Individual

Offers integrative grief therapy combining Cognitive Behavioral Therapy, mindfulness, somatic techniques, and Acceptance and Commitment Therapy. Especially helpful for complicated grief, prolonged grief disorder, or grief combined with anxiety or depression. Insurance accepted.

(415) 310-5142

Sutter Health Bereavement Program

Free Open to All

Free individual and group bereavement support serving San Francisco and Marin counties. Open to anyone grieving the loss of a loved one — you do not need to be a Sutter Health patient. Groups are facilitated by trained bereavement counselors.

(855) 259-0943

What Grief Can Look and Feel Like

There is no "right" way to grieve. But understanding what you may experience can help you be gentler with yourself.

"You might feel numb"

In the first days and weeks, many people feel nothing at all — or feel like they're watching themselves from the outside. This is your mind protecting you. It's normal and it will shift in its own time.

"You might feel angry"

Anger is one of the most common — and most misunderstood — grief responses. You might feel angry at the person who died, at doctors, at God, at yourself, or at the world. This is grief, not a character flaw.

"You might feel relief"

If your loved one suffered a long illness, feeling relief doesn't mean you didn't love them. It means you loved them enough to not want them to suffer. Relief and grief can coexist.

"Grief has no timeline"

Anyone who tells you "it's been long enough" has never lost what you've lost. Grief comes in waves — it may feel manageable for weeks and then hit you in the grocery store on a Tuesday. This is normal.

"Physical symptoms are real"

Grief affects the body too: exhaustion, insomnia, appetite changes, chest tightness, brain fog, and weakened immunity are all documented grief responses. If symptoms persist, please see your doctor.

Further Reading

"It's OK That You're Not OK"

by Megan Devine

A compassionate guide that validates grief rather than trying to fix it.

"The Year of Magical Thinking"

by Joan Didion

A powerful memoir of sudden loss and the mind's attempt to make sense of it.

"Option B"

by Sheryl Sandberg & Adam Grant

Practical wisdom on building resilience after loss.

"When Breath Becomes Air"

by Paul Kalanithi

A neurosurgeon's reflection on mortality and meaning.
